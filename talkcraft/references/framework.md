# Framework

## Objective

This skill turns presentation craft into a repeatable agent workflow. The framework centers on disciplined promise-setting, memorable structure, explicit transitions, and concrete evidence.

## Core Principles

### Promise First

Every talk needs a clear promise. The audience should know why the talk matters and what they will gain. If the promise stays muddy, the rest of the talk drifts.

### Slogan

Reduce the talk to one compact governing line. This becomes the thread that holds sections together and helps the speaker reject weak detours.

### Fences

Divide the talk into a small number of named sections. A good fence isolates one job. Audiences track structure when the sections feel deliberate and the speaker marks transitions clearly.

Each fence should also own one demonstration choice:

- story
- example
- board move
- sketch
- contrast
- demo
- number

### Verbal Punctuation

Listeners cannot scroll backward. Good speakers state where the talk is, what just happened, and what comes next.

### Concrete Before Abstract

Most talks fail from abstraction overload. Prefer examples, demos, contrasts, stories, or drawings when a concept gets slippery.

### Opening Discipline

Open with relevance and direction. A strong opening does four things quickly:

1. names the problem or stakes
2. tells the audience why they should care
3. states the promise
4. creates curiosity for the path ahead

### Strong Close

Close by compressing the full argument into one clean recap and one clear implication. End before the talk dissolves into apology, weak thanks, or an unplanned question slide.

### Rehearsal As Design

Do not treat rehearsal as a last cosmetic pass. Rehearsal exposes timing failures, muddy transitions, and overloaded slides. Use rehearsal feedback to restructure, not just memorize.

## Section Design

For each fence, define:

- time allocation
- question answered
- one memory target
- evidence type
- board, demo, or visual move
- transition line

If any fence cannot answer these six items, the section probably lacks focus.

## Timing Guidance

- Opening: roughly `10-15%`
- Core fences: roughly `70-80%`
- Close plus Q&A setup: roughly `10-15%`

For short talks, reduce the number of fences before shrinking transitions. Structure matters more than density.

## Common Failure Modes

- title without promise
- too many equal-weight bullets
- no audience prior-knowledge assumption
- examples delayed too late
- transitions left implicit
- close weaker than opening
- no timing budget
- no cut line for time pressure
- no likely-question preparation

## Adaptation Rules

### Executive Briefing

- lead with decision and consequence
- compress technical depth
- emphasize tradeoffs

### Thesis Defense

- lead with research problem and claim
- isolate contribution from background
- pre-answer obvious objections

### Product Demo

- promise outcome before feature list
- use one narrative path
- keep demo transitions verbal, not silent

### Lecture Or Teaching Session

- repeat the roadmap more often
- interleave examples earlier
- recap each fence before moving on
